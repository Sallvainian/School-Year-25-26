## COMMON UNDERSTANDING PROFILES

### Profile A: Concrete Observers
**Characteristics**:
- Strong observation skills
- Detailed descriptions of what they see
- Difficulty making connections between trials
- Focus on surface features rather than underlying mechanisms

**Evidence**:
- T-charts with many specific observations
- Models show accurate before/after states
- Struggles with "why" questions
- Limited use of force/energy concepts

**Instructional Response**:
- Use comparison protocols: "How is this trial different from the last one?"
- Provide sentence stems: "This happened because..."
- Connect observations to causes: "When you saw X, what do you think caused it?"
- Partner with students who make connections easily

### Profile B: Pattern Seekers
**Characteristics**:
- Naturally groups similar observations
- Identifies variables affecting outcomes
- Beginning to make predictions
- May jump to conclusions without sufficient evidence

**Evidence**:
- Well-organized T-charts with clear categories
- Models show some causal relationships
- Makes predictions but doesn't always test them
- Uses some scientific vocabulary

**Instructional Response**:
- Encourage evidence collection before conclusion-making
- Ask: "What evidence supports that pattern?"
- Provide opportunities to test predictions
- Challenge with exception cases

### Profile C: Mechanistic Thinkers
**Characteristics**:
- Attempts to explain how things work
- Uses force and energy language
- May have sophisticated misconceptions
- Connects to prior knowledge extensively

**Evidence**:
- Models include arrows, forces, or energy representations
- Explanations focus on mechanisms
- May over-complicate simple phenomena
- Shows evidence of physics misconceptions

**Instructional Response**:
- Validate mechanistic thinking while correcting misconceptions
- Use phenomena to test explanations
- Ask: "How could we test that explanation?"
- Provide accurate scientific models for comparison

### Profile D: Struggling Engagers
**Characteristics**:
- Difficulty staying focused on investigation
- Limited observation recording
- Relies heavily on others for ideas
- May lack confidence in scientific thinking

**Evidence**:
- Sparse or copied T-chart entries
- Models lack detail or explanation
- Minimal participation in discussions
- Shows frustration with open-ended tasks

**Instructional Response**:
- Provide structured observation templates
- Use think-pair-share before individual work
- Celebrate small victories and specific contributions
- Offer multiple ways to demonstrate understanding

## MISCONCEPTION IDENTIFICATION & RESPONSE

### Force and Motion Misconceptions

**Misconception 1**: "Heavier objects always exert more force"
```
EVIDENCE TO WATCH FOR:
- Students predict heavy ball always "wins"
- Focus only on mass when explaining outcomes
- Ignore speed/direction factors

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS:
- "What if the small ball is moving very fast?"
- "Which has more effect: weight or speed?"
- "How could a small object stop a large one?"

INSTRUCTIONAL RESPONSE:
- Test scenarios with different speeds
- Compare throwing force vs. object weight
- Use everyday examples (small hammer, large pillow)
```

**Misconception 2**: "Objects stop moving because they "want to" or "run out of force""
```
EVIDENCE TO WATCH FOR:
- Anthropomorphic language (ball "wants to stop")
- Ideas about objects having limited "force supply"
- No mention of friction or external forces

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS:
- "What makes the ball stop rolling?"
- "Why doesn't it keep moving forever?"
- "What's pushing on the ball when it slows down?"

INSTRUCTIONAL RESPONSE:
- Investigate different surfaces and friction
- Demonstrate motion continuation (rolling on smooth surfaces)
- Explicitly discuss friction as an opposing force
```

**Misconception 3**: "Only one object exerts force in a collision"
```
EVIDENCE TO WATCH FOR:
- Models show force arrows on only one object
- Language like "the ball hits the other ball"
- No recognition of mutual interaction

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS:
- "Which ball feels the collision?"
- "What pushes back on the moving ball?"
- "How do both balls change motion?"

INSTRUCTIONAL RESPONSE:
- Use action-reaction demonstrations
- Have students feel forces when pushing walls
- Analyze slow-motion collision videos
```

## DAILY DATA ANALYSIS ROUTINE

### 5-Minute Post-Lesson Analysis

**Step 1: Quick Sort** (2 minutes)
Sort student work or exit tickets into three piles:
- **Green**: Shows proficient understanding
- **Yellow**: Shows developing understanding with some gaps
- **Red**: Shows significant confusion or misconceptions

**Step 2: Pattern Recognition** (2 minutes)
Identify patterns across the class:
- What did most students understand well?
- What concept showed the most confusion?
- Which misconceptions appeared multiple times?

**Step 3: Tomorrow's Focus** (1 minute)
Based on patterns, decide:
- What concept needs reteaching or reinforcement?
- Which students need individual check-ins?
- What misconceptions need addressing?

### Weekly Analysis Protocol

**Monday: Review Previous Week's Growth**
- Compare exit tickets from beginning to end of week
- Identify students showing significant progress
- Note persistent misconceptions for targeted intervention

**Wednesday: Mid-Week Adjustment Check**
- Analyze discussion participation patterns
- Review model development progression
- Adjust small group compositions based on understanding

**Friday: Preparation for Next Week**
- Synthesize formative evidence for report cards/conferences
- Plan differentiated activities based on understanding levels
- Prepare targeted support materials for struggling students

## INTERVENTION STRATEGIES BY UNDERSTANDING LEVEL

### Tier 1: Whole Class Interventions
**When**: >50% of students show similar gaps

**Strategy Options**:
```
MISCONCEPTION REVEALED: Objects stop because they "run out of force"
INTERVENTION:
- Whole class investigation of rolling balls on different surfaces
- Explicit friction instruction with hands-on examples
- Compare motion in air vs. on surfaces

PATTERN CONFUSION: Students can't identify collision factors
INTERVENTION:
- Gallery walk to share T-chart strategies
- Structured comparison of successful vs. struggling examples
- Collaborative creation of class "collision factors" poster
```

### Tier 2: Small Group Interventions
**When**: 20-50% of students need similar support

**Group Configurations**:
```
OBSERVATION SUPPORT GROUP (Profile A + D):
- Structured observation sheets with guided questions
- Partner with strong observers
- Practice describing observations aloud before writing

PATTERN CONNECTION GROUP (Profile A â†’ B transition):
- Comparison activities with guided questions
- Cause-effect graphic organizers
- Practice making predictions with immediate testing

MISCONCEPTION TARGETED GROUP (Profile C with specific errors):
- Direct instruction on correct scientific concepts
- Hands-on testing of misconceptions
- Explicit comparison of student ideas vs. scientific explanations
```

### Tier 3: Individual Interventions
**When**: <20% of students need intensive support

**Individual Strategies**:
```
CONFIDENCE BUILDING:
- One-on-one explanation opportunities
- Success tracking charts
- Choice in how to demonstrate understanding

SKILL BUILDING:
- Explicit instruction in observation techniques
- Vocabulary development with visual supports
- Step-by-step modeling protocols

ACCELERATION:
- Independent investigation projects
- Mentoring struggling peers
- Research and presentation opportunities
```
